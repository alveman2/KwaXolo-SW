# Interaction Patterns

These are the standard patterns for how students interact with content. Use the right pattern for the right content type.

---

## Pattern 1 — Step-by-step guided task

**When to use:** Any task that involves using a real app or tool (Gmail, WhatsApp Business, Canva, Facebook, Google Maps, etc.)

**How it works:**
- The student is guided through the real app, one step at a time
- Each step produces something they can see or show (a new account, a message sent, a photo taken)
- The platform shows one step at a time — not the full list

**Try-and-fail principle:** The student is expected to try, get something slightly wrong, and try again. Steps should be written clearly enough that the student can recover from a mistake without help. Add a one-line tip on any step where a common mistake is predictable.

Example tip: *"If you see an error, check that your password has at least 8 characters and one number."*

---

## Pattern 2 — Write and reflect

**When to use:** Any task where the student creates their own content (a business description, a customer list, a pricing table)

**How it works:**
- The student is given a clear prompt and a text area
- They write their answer and submit
- Their answer is saved to their Business Profile on the platform
- The teacher can see submitted answers

**Rules:**
- Prompts must be specific, not open-ended: "Write the names of three people in your family or neighbourhood who spend money regularly on food, transport, or services" is better than "Write about potential customers."
- The student should be able to complete the writing in under 5 minutes

---

## Pattern 3 — Try this now (live task)

**When to use:** Tasks where the student does something real and observable during the session (sends a WhatsApp to a contact, creates a real profile, takes a product photo)

**How it works:**
- The step says "do this now, on your real phone"
- The task is grounded in the student's actual life — not a simulation
- The reflection question asks what happened when they tried

**Rules:**
- Only use when the action is safe, free, and reversible
- Never ask a student to share personal information with strangers or post publicly without understanding what they're doing
- If the task involves sending a message, write the exact message text the student can copy and send

---

## Pattern 4 — Explore and observe

**When to use:** Tasks where the student navigates somewhere new and reports back (Facebook Marketplace, Google Maps, a competitor's page)

**How it works:**
- The student opens a real app or website
- They look for something specific and write down what they find
- The reflection question asks them to analyse what they found

**Example:** "Open Facebook Marketplace on your phone. Search for 'food' in your local area. Write down the name and price of one food item someone is selling near you."

---

## Fail-recovery design

Every task with a technical step should anticipate common failure modes:
- Forgot password → tell them how to reset it
- App not installed → tell them where to find it
- Slow internet → tell them to try on school WiFi
- Step looks different on their phone → acknowledge that apps change and give a search term to find the right button

Do not write "ask your teacher" as the only fallback. The student should be able to continue independently.

## App-by-app step templates

Use these as the starting point when writing steps for these common apps. Adapt to fit the lesson — do not copy blindly.

### WhatsApp

```
1. Open WhatsApp on your phone.
   The icon is a green square with a white phone and speech bubble.

2. Tap the green pencil button at the bottom right of the screen.
   You should see your contact list.

3. Find the person you want to message. Tap their name.
   You should see a blank message box at the bottom of the screen.

4. Tap the message box and type your message.
   Do not send it yet — check that it is clear first.

5. Tap the green send button on the right side of the message box.
   You should see your message appear on the screen with a tick.
```

### Phone camera

```
1. Open the Camera app on your phone.
   Look for the camera icon on your home screen.

2. Point the camera at the item you want to photograph.
   Hold the phone still with both hands.

3. Tap the large round button to take the photo.
   You should see the photo appear for a second on your screen.

4. Open your Gallery app to find the photo.
   Check that the image is clear and the words are readable.
```

### Notes app

```
1. Open the Notes app on your phone.
   If you do not have one, open Google Keep or Samsung Notes.

2. Tap the button for a new note. It usually looks like a + or a pencil.

3. Type the name of your idea at the top as the title.

4. Write three short lines below it.

5. Tap Save or the back arrow to save the note.
   You should see your note listed when you go back to the home screen of the app.
```

### Gmail (new account setup)

```
1. Open the Play Store on your phone.
   Tap the search bar at the top and type "Gmail".

2. Tap the Gmail app in the results. Tap "Install" if it is not on your phone yet.
   Wait for it to download. This takes about 1 minute.

3. Open Gmail. Tap "Create account" then tap "For myself".

4. Enter your first name and last name. Tap "Next".

5. Choose a Gmail address. Use your real name if possible
   — for example: thandi.nzama@gmail.com. Tap "Next".

6. Create a strong password. Use at least 8 characters.
   Write it down somewhere safe. Tap "Next".

7. Follow the remaining screens. Tap "Agree" when asked.
   You should see your Gmail inbox when setup is complete.
```

### School PC (browser-based task)

```
1. Open the web browser on the computer.
   Look for Chrome, Firefox, or Internet Explorer on the desktop.

2. Open the saved lesson file from the desktop or USB drive.
   The file name ends in .html.

3. Click the section called "Student Task".

4. Click the empty answer box and type your response.

5. Check that your answer is saved or written down before you close the page.
```
