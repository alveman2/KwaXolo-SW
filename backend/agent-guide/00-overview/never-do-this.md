# Never Do This

A complete list of what the agent must never do, plus bad output examples that show why these rules exist.

---

## The never-do list

### Content and structure
- Never produce only one of the two required outputs
- Never add extra unrequested sections
- Never skip steps that seem obvious — assume the student has never done this before
- Never end a student task step without a visible, confirmable result

### Language
- Never use business school jargon (see `05-language-guide/english-standard.md` for the full list)
- Never use idioms that non-native English speakers would not understand
- Never write student steps that only say "think", "consider", "understand", or "explore"
- Never use long dense paragraphs in student tasks

### Context
- Never give examples set in Johannesburg, Cape Town, New York, London, or Silicon Valley unless specifically asked
- Never use a generic "small business" example when a named KZN reference fits
- Never assume students have a car, a formal business space, a printer, or a laptop of their own

### Money and devices
- Never suggest students need money to begin a business
- Never build student tasks that require paid apps or paid accounts
- Never build student tasks that require live internet without providing an offline fallback
- Never tell the teacher to use slides, a projector, or a screen for the class

### Teaching approach
- Never make the teacher sound like an entrepreneurship expert
- Never use "motivational speaker" language or abstract inspiration
- Never recommend ventures that require startup capital as step one
- Never give student steps that could be completed by just sitting still

## Bad output examples (with fixes)

### Bad example 1 — Vague student step

**Bad:**
```
1. Think about your target market.
```

**Why it fails:**
- No physical action
- Uses jargon ("target market")
- No visible result
- Can be "completed" by staring at the desk

**Fixed:**
```
1. Write the names of three people near your home who might buy your product.
   Think about people who have a problem your product solves.
   You should have three names written on your page.
```

---

### Bad example 2 — Generic local example

**Bad:**
> A young entrepreneur in a rural area started a small food business by selling homemade goods to neighbours.

**Why it fails:**
- No name, no place, no real detail
- Could apply to anywhere in the world
- The teacher cannot build on it or verify it

**Fixed:**
> Thabo Shude started 1LT Bakery by selling bread to neighbours in the community. His first customers were people who passed his home on the way to school and to the local tuck shop. He started in his kitchen — no shop, no startup loan.

---

### Bad example 3 — Teacher explanation with jargon

**Bad:**
> Today we will examine the strategic fundamentals of entrepreneurial thinking. Students must understand that a viable business model requires a clear value proposition, a defined customer segment, and a scalable revenue stream. We will leverage local examples to contextualise these theoretical frameworks.

**Why it fails:**
- Jargon in every sentence
- No Grade 8 student can follow this
- The teacher cannot read this aloud without sounding confused
- No warmth, no practical grounding

**Fixed:**
> Today we are going to talk about how to find the right person to sell to.
>
> Before you sell anything, it helps to think about who would actually want to buy it. Not everyone will want your product — and that is fine. You are looking for the people who have the problem your product solves.
>
> If you bake bread, your customers are people who want to eat bread but do not have time to bake it. If you fix phones, your customers are people with cracked screens or slow apps. You do not need to reach everyone. You just need to find the right few.

---

### Bad example 4 — Task that needs money and internet

**Bad:**
```
1. Open Canva on your phone and log in to your account.
2. Choose a professional template for your business flyer.
3. Upload your brand logo and product photos.
4. Purchase the premium version to remove the watermark.
5. Download and share your finished flyer on Facebook Ads.
```

**Why it fails:**
- Requires internet and a Canva account
- Requires a brand logo (students do not have one)
- Requires money (Canva premium, Facebook Ads)
- No offline fallback

**Fixed:**
```
1. Open your notebook. Draw a rectangle in the middle of the page — this is your flyer.

2. Write the name of your product or service at the top in large letters.

3. Write the price clearly below the name.

4. Write one sentence that explains why someone should buy from you.

5. Add your WhatsApp number at the bottom.
   You should have a simple hand-drawn flyer you can show to people or photograph and send on WhatsApp.
```
