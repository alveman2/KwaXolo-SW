# Category A — Starting a Business

## A1: Finding a business idea in your community

**Student task type:** Explore and observe  
**Interaction pattern:** The student walks around or thinks about their community and writes down what they observe

**Local grounding hooks:**
- Spaza shops as existing reference points for local demand
- Common needs: food, hair services, phone repair, printing, transport, tutoring
- Chief Inkosi Xolo's community as the physical space students know
- Msenti Hub as a place where local business ideas have turned into real ventures

**Hard rules:**
- Never suggest ideas that require startup capital (no "buy stock to resell" without acknowledging zero-capital constraint)
- Always include at least one idea that requires only a skill, not materials (tutoring, hair, repairs, delivery)

## A2: Registering your business with SEDA

**Student task type:** Write and reflect  
**Interaction pattern:** Student researches SEDA online or writes what they would do to register a business

**Local grounding hooks:**
- SEDA Port Shepstone — specific, free, government-backed
- Caleb Phehlukwayo (former principal) as a community figure who understands formal processes
- Msenti Hub as a place that helps with registration support

**Hard rules:**
- Be specific: SEDA Port Shepstone, not "a government office"
- Acknowledge that registration takes time — this is not a one-day task
- Always mention that SEDA is free — cost is a common barrier

## A3: Writing a simple business plan (Lean Canvas)

**Student task type:** Write and reflect  
**Interaction pattern:** Student fills in a simplified Lean Canvas — 5 fields maximum

**Local grounding hooks:**
- Lean Canvas simplified to: problem, customers, solution, revenue, unfair advantage
- Use 1LT Bakery (Thabo Shude) or Hlobisile Pearl Studios as worked examples
- Frame the canvas as a one-page document, not a formal business plan

**Hard rules:**
- Never use the full Lean Canvas 9-box format — it is too complex
- Use plain language for each field: "What problem do you solve?" not "Problem Statement"
- Student task should produce a real, partially filled canvas for their own idea

## A4: Setting your first prices

**Student task type:** Write and reflect  
**Interaction pattern:** Student calculates a price for a hypothetical or real product/service

**Local grounding hooks:**
- MTN Mobile Money as the payment context
- Spaza shop pricing as a reference point students understand
- Inkify (printing business) as an example of pricing a service, not a product

**Hard rules:**
- Start with cost + time, not "what competitors charge" — students often have no visible competitors
- Always address the discomfort of asking for money: "It feels awkward to charge friends. That is normal. Here is how to handle it."
- Never suggest undercutting on price as a first strategy — it trains bad habits

## A5: Finding your first 10 customers

**Student task type:** Write and reflect + Try this now  
**Interaction pattern:** Student writes a list of 10 real people and how they would approach each one

**Local grounding hooks:**
- WhatsApp as the tool for reaching first customers
- Family, neighbours, school as the first circles — "warm network" concept without jargon
- Hlobisile Pearl Studios found its first clients through word of mouth in the community

**Hard rules:**
- Frame this as a people exercise, not a marketing exercise
- Students should write real names if they feel comfortable — not "hypothetical customer A"
- End with a step where the student actually sends one WhatsApp message if they are ready
