# Category E — Sector-Specific Businesses

All Category E modules share one rule: every example must come from the specific sector, not generic entrepreneurship advice. A student choosing E1 is already thinking about food — give them food-specific, KZN-specific content.

---

## E1: Starting a food business (baking, catering, preserves)

**Student task type:** Write and reflect  
**Interaction pattern:** Student writes a mini plan for a food product they could actually make

**Local grounding hooks:**
- 1LT Bakery (Thabo Shude) — started with zero capital, sells to neighbours
- Common zero-capital food options: vetkoek, amagwinya, umngqusho, catering for events, cold drinks at school
- Health considerations: mention that selling food from home in South Africa requires a basic food safety awareness (no license required for informal selling, but hygiene matters)
- MTN Mobile Money and Capitec for taking payments

**Hard rules:**
- Only zero-capital or very low-capital food ideas (use existing kitchen, ingredients bought per order)
- Always include hygiene as one of the "Write on the Board" points
- Never suggest a large investment (buy a fridge, rent a commercial kitchen) without flagging the cost

## E2: Starting a services business (photography, printing, tutoring)

**Student task type:** Write and reflect  
**Interaction pattern:** Student identifies one service they could offer based on a skill they already have

**Local grounding hooks:**
- Hlobisile Pearl Studios (photography and events) — started with a phone camera
- Inkify (printing) — started with one printer
- Tutoring younger students: zero capital, uses existing knowledge
- Hair services: widely practised informally, low or no startup cost

**Hard rules:**
- Always start with: "What can you already do that other people would pay for?" — skill-first framing
- Do not assume the student has equipment — first skill, then tool
- Tutoring must be included as an option — it requires zero equipment and is overlooked

## E3: Starting an agri-business (vegetable farming, poultry)

**Student task type:** Write and reflect  
**Interaction pattern:** Student identifies a crop or animal product they could produce and who would buy it

**Local grounding hooks:**
- KwaZulu-Natal has strong agri-tech growth (7.6% CAGR)
- Agri-business as a family activity — many students already help at home with small gardens
- Msenti Hub has supported agri-entrepreneurs in the community
- Chief Inkosi Xolo's traditional community context — land use is a community decision

**Hard rules:**
- Never recommend poultry without acknowledging the feed cost — this is not truly zero-capital
- Vegetable growing from seeds is the lowest-capital option — lead with this
- Connect to existing buyers: spaza shops, families, school canteens
- Land access must be addressed: "Do you have a small piece of land or garden space?"

## E4: Starting a trade business (repairs, construction, sewing)

**Student task type:** Write and reflect  
**Interaction pattern:** Student identifies a trade skill (or one to learn) and their first potential customer

**Local grounding hooks:**
- Construction and trade: 5.2% CAGR in KZN
- Phone repair as a growing opportunity — most students encounter broken phones
- Sewing and alterations: existing skill in many households, minimal startup cost
- Caleb Phehlukwayo (former principal) as a community figure who would know people needing repairs

**Hard rules:**
- Trade skills take time to learn — acknowledge learning curve, do not oversimplify
- Focus on apprenticeship and observation as a starting point: "Find someone who already does this and learn from them first"
- Phone repair is appropriate for high school students — do not dismiss as too technical

## E5: Selling crafts and handmade products online

**Student task type:** Step-by-step guided task  
**Interaction pattern:** Student takes a photo of a craft item and creates a WhatsApp or Facebook listing

**Local grounding hooks:**
- Artisan and food products: 6.1% CAGR in KZN
- Traditional Zulu crafts have national and international markets (beadwork, baskets, woodwork)
- Facebook Marketplace as the primary local channel; WhatsApp groups for immediate community sales
- Hlobisile Pearl Studios helps document and photograph handmade work for clients

**Hard rules:**
- First step: create the product (or describe one they already make)
- Second step: photograph it properly (refer to C3 lesson if available)
- Third step: write a product description with price
- Never suggest shipping without a viable way to receive payment first — cash on collection is safest to start
