# Teacher Lesson Plan Structure

Every lesson plan the agent generates follows this exact structure. The teacher uses it with chalk and blackboard only — no screen, no projector.

---

## The 7 sections

### 1. Lesson Title

Short, plain language. Maximum 8 words. No jargon.

| Good | Bad |
|---|---|
| How to Find Your First 10 Customers | Customer Acquisition and Market Penetration Strategies |
| Sell With WhatsApp | Leveraging Digital Channels for Business Growth |
| Track Money In and Out | Financial Record Management and Bookkeeping |
| Start a Small Business | Entrepreneurial Value Creation |

---

### 2. Learning Objective

One sentence. Written from the student's perspective: "After this lesson, you will be able to..."

**Good:**
> After this lesson, you will be able to describe three people in your community who might pay for your product.

**Bad:**
> Students will understand customer segmentation and value proposition alignment.

Why bad: too broad, not practical, cannot be checked, uses jargon.

---

### 3. Write on the Board

3–5 bullet points only. Maximum 6 words per point. These are the only words that go on the blackboard. The teacher writes these at the start of the lesson. Must fit on a blackboard in under 2 minutes.

**Good:**
```
- A customer is someone who pays
- Find people with a problem
- Ask before you sell
- Your first customer is near you
- WhatsApp is a free way to reach them
```

**Bad:**
```
- Entrepreneurs must develop a sustainable and scalable business model that creates long-term value for target customers in their local ecosystem.
```

Why bad: too long, too difficult, uses jargon, cannot fit on a blackboard.

---

### 4. Explain to Students

300–400 words. Written as a script the teacher reads aloud and explains in their own words. Must be:
- Plain language, no jargon
- Short sentences (max 20 words per sentence)
- Structure: introduce the idea → explain → give an example → connect to student life
- One real local KwaZulu-Natal example embedded in this section
- Warm, guiding tone — the teacher sounds like a guide, not a lecturer

**Good tone:**
> Today we are going to look at how a small business can begin with a simple problem in the community.

**Bad tone:**
> Today we will interrogate the strategic foundations of entrepreneurial value creation and contextualise key theoretical frameworks within a rural KwaZulu-Natal context.

---

### 5. Discussion Questions

Exactly 3 questions. Numbered. Open-ended — no yes/no questions. Designed to prompt students to connect the lesson to their own life. Simple enough for a shy student to answer aloud.

**Good:**
```
1. What is one problem people near your home often have?
2. What could a young person do to help solve that problem?
3. How could WhatsApp help someone tell others about this service?
```

**Bad:**
```
1. Is business important?
2. Do you want to be rich?
3. What is ROI?
```

Why bad: questions 1 and 2 are yes/no, question 3 uses jargon and cannot be answered by a Grade 8 student.

---

### 6. Local Example

One dedicated example block. Specific — name a real place, person, or venture from KwaZulu-Natal. Must connect directly to the lesson topic.

**Good:**
> Thabo Shude started 1LT Bakery by selling bread to neighbours. His first customers were people who walked past his home every morning. He did not need a shop — he started in his kitchen with what he already had.

**Bad:**
> A young entrepreneur in a rural area started a small baking business by selling to neighbours.

Why bad: no name, no place, no real detail, could apply anywhere in the world.

Approved references to use:
- Msenti Entrepreneurship Hub (Victor Jaca)
- SEDA Port Shepstone
- Dolly Dlezi (accountant)
- Caleb Phehlukwayo (community leader)
- Chief Inkosi Xolo (traditional authority)
- 1LT Bakery (Thabo Shude)
- Hlobisile Pearl Studios
- Inkify (Samke Jaca and Ntokozo Gwacela)
- Capitec, MTN Mobile Money, FNB eWallet

---

### 7. Time Guide

A simple breakdown of how to use the available class time. Must fit within 30–45 minutes total.

**Good:**
```
5 min: write board points, students copy
15 min: teacher explains
10 min: class discussion
5 min: recap and hand out student task
```

## Print and display rules

- Teacher lesson plans are output as HTML on the platform (for on-screen reading) and as a print-ready PDF
- PDF must be readable when printed in black and white on a standard printer
- Font size minimum 14pt in print format
- Each section separated by a clear heading
- The "Write on the Board" section should be visually distinct — boxed or bold — so the teacher can find it instantly
- See `07-output-formats/teacher-pdf-spec.md` for the full HTML/CSS template
